I am an architect and educator based in Las Vegas — currently at Klai Juba Wald Architects + Interiors, contributing to landmark hospitality projects including the Guitar Tower at Hard Rock Las Vegas, LVCC Phase III, Hard Rock Athens, and Hard Rock San Juan.

I work two sides of the same craft: the precise, often invisible detailing that gets big, complicated buildings out of the ground, and the slower research that keeps asking why we build the way we do. I hold dual M.Arch degrees in Hospitality & Entertainment Design (UNLV) and Digital Architecture Technology (Pars University, Tehran).

As Instructor at UNLV's College of Architecture, Planning, and Public Affairs, I teach architectural design studios while actively pursuing ARE licensure.

Right now — on the boards for the Guitar Tower at Hard Rock Las Vegas, and teaching second-year studio at UNLV.

Video 01 · Group Project

Kinetic Sun-Shader — A Façade That Reads the Light

A collaborative experiment in responsive architecture: a kinetic shading screen that measures the sun's intensity and reshapes itself in real time to temper the light it lets through. The geometry was driven by a Grasshopper definition wired to an Arduino sensing rig, and the working prototype was built entirely from cardboard — a deliberately low-fi study of a high-performance idea.

Teaching · UNLV School of Architecture

Inside the studio.

Alongside practice, I teach and mentor design students at UNLV's School of Architecture — guiding projects from first concept through final review. For most of these students this is their first second-year studio, and the first building they've ever designed start to finish: a window into how the next generation learns to reason through site, structure, and story.

Fall 2025 Design Foundation II UNLV | AAE 280 · PROJECT 05

I mentored this studio through its final design response — the capstone of the semester. Students took a site they'd analyzed earlier in the term and resolved it into a complete hospitality retreat: a cluster of cabins, a café, and an event space. The brief pushed them to settle site layout and building placement, resolve roof forms and exterior materials, develop repeatable moves for recurring conditions, and keep all circulation ADA-compliant — produced entirely by hand, in drawings and a monochromatic study model, with no 3D software.

Final Design Response

The Yucca

Dann Carlo Ramos

Dann's arts-and-culture center in Albuquerque is organized as a climate-responsive landscape, its form borrowed from the yucca — "as it goes up, it goes outward." Earth-sheltered volumes, a rattan shading canopy, and terracotta thermal-mass walls wrap a semi-sunken amphitheater, balancing passive cooling with community gathering.

Spring 2026 Design Foundation III UNLV | AAE 282 · PROJECT 04

This studio worked from a shared group master plan — "Fledgling Future" — for the Wildlife Center of the North Coast, a real wildlife-rehabilitation center on the Pacific Northwest coast. I guided each student as they took a single piece of that plan and developed it into a complete individual project, this time pairing hand drawing with digital rendering and thinking in modular, prefabricable terms suited to a remote site.

Individual Project

Hand-Grown Sanctuary

Victoria Jungic

Victoria framed the whole center around a single idea — a sanctuary that is "cultivated, not constructed," growing and healing like the animals it shelters. From a full site-zoning study she developed a family of bird blinds — central, children's, riverside, and stream — with one-way viewing film, integrated perches, and guano collection, worked out in watercolor elevations and cardboard study models.

Individual Project

Flowform

Jacob Miranda

Jacob's kinetic education workshop runs on its own water wheel — water turns the wheel, the shaft drives a generator, and wiring carries power onto the site. A kinetic aluminum façade reads as "birds in flight," Shou Sugi Ban charred-cedar walls push the building's lifespan past fifty years, and bird-safe glazing protects the wildlife it teaches about. The most render-forward project in the studio, paired with a glowing study model.

Signage Improvements

Evan Phillips

Evan saw the center's mismatched signage as an opportunity and built a modular system to fix it — inspired by "Cormie," WCNC's ambassador cormorant. A timber-and-aluminum entry gateway, info panels, wayfinding posts, and a new ambassador-animal series are all worked out to section cuts, exploded axons, materials, and a per-sign budget, then composited back onto real site photos.

Individual Project

Fledgling Future: Trails

Esmeralda Gonzalez

Esmeralda designed the master plan's connective tissue — a trail network stitched together with seating, restrooms, and gathering spaces. Reading how social seabirds flock, she shaped places for people to do the same: a sculptural, kidney-plan event space in rough-cut stone, steel, and tinted concrete, and an outdoor "convo-pit," a sunken circular conversation seat in timber and concrete, all carried in watercolor.
